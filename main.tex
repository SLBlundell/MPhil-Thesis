\section{The Model}

I present an endowment economy inhabited by three distinct agents: a Representative Household (the sovereign), a continuum of risk-neutral Domestic Import Intermediaries (Domestic Banks) indexed by $i\in[0,1]$, and a risk-neutral International Lender. 

\subsection{Sequence of Events}
The discrete-time model spans two periods, $t \in \{0,1\}$, with the following sub-period timeline:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Period 0:} 
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The household receives endowment $y_0$.
        \item The household issues intertemporal bonds $b_1$ at price $q_0$. A fraction $\gamma$ is purchased by domestic intermediaries; the rest by the international lender.
        \item Intermediaries borrow working capital $\kappa_0$ from the international lender to intermediate foreign inputs $m^f_0$.
        \item The household produces and consumes final aggregate $C_0$.
    \end{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Period 1:}
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The aggregate endowment shock $g_1$ is realised, determining $y_1$.
        \item The household chooses the partial default level $D \in [0, b_1]$.
        \item Domestic intermediaries' net worth $n_{i,1}$ is updated based on the post-default value of their bond holdings.
        \item Intermediaries borrow working capital $\kappa_1$ subject to a Costly State Verification (CSV) contract, determining the domestic price of foreign inputs $p_{m,1}$.
        \item The idiosyncratic shock $\omega_{i,1}$ is realised; solvent intermediaries deliver inputs.
        \item The household produces and consumes final aggregate $C_1$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Domestic Household}

The representative household acts as a consumer and final goods producer, aggregating domestic ($m^d_t$) and foreign ($m^f_t$) inputs to produce a final consumption good $C_t$. They take the internal price of foreign inputs, $p_{m,t}$, as given in each period $t$. The price of domestic inputs acts as a the world numéraire, with $p_{m,t}$ defined as the relative price expressed in units of domestic inputs $m^d$. \\

The Household funds its consumption via its per-period endowment $y_t$, and by issuing one-period bonds $b_t$ to intertemporally smooth consumption, acting as claims on future consumption. As an important departure from models of sovereign default such as \citet{arellano_default_2008}, the household does not internalise the effect their defaulting decision has on the price of debt. Rather, default in this model occurs in equilibrium as households internalise the impact of default on import prices. The sovereign in this setting can be thought of as being subject to a coordination failure in the issuance of new debt \citep{goodhart_debt_2018}.

\subsubsection*{Period 0}
The Domestic Household seeks to maximise lifetime utility $V_0$,
\begin{equation} \label{eq: lifetime utility}
    V_0 = \max_{m^d_0, \, m^f_0, \, b_1} \quad u(C_0) + \beta \mathbb{E}_0 [u(C_1)],
\end{equation}
subject to the Period 0 budget constraint,
\begin{equation} \label{eq: period 0 bc}
    m^d_0 + p_{m,0} m^f_0 = y_0 - b_0 + q_0 b_1,
\end{equation}
where $q_0$ is the price of household bonds, set by the international lender in Period 0, and the CES production aggregator,
\begin{equation} \label{eq: CES aggregator}
    C_t = \left[ \alpha (m^d_t)^\eta + (1-\alpha) (m^f_t)^\eta \right]^{\frac{1}{\eta}}, \quad 
    \text{for } t \in \{0,1\}, \text{ where } \eta<0,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the Armington weight of domestic inputs, and $\eta$ is the Armington elasticity of substitution between domestic and foreign inputs. It is worth noting that the two inputs are strictly complementary; the household should not be able to substitute away from foreign inputs to avoid punitive price increases in these inputs.

\subsubsection*{Period 1}

The economy is subject to an aggregate endowment shock in Period 1, the growth of which is governed by an auto-regressive process of order one,
\begin{equation} \label{eq: endowment shock}
    \log(g_1) = (1-\rho)\log(\mu_g) + \rho\log(g_0) + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2_{g}),
\end{equation}
where,
\begin{equation} \label{eq: period 1 endowment}
    y_1 = g_1 y_0.
\end{equation}

Upon realisation of $y_1$, the sovereign household chooses continuous partial default $D$, interpreted as the haircut the household receives from the international lender, and Period 1 inputs to maximise utility:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: period 1 default problem}
    \max_{D \in [0, b_1], \, m^d_1 \geq 0, \, m^f_1 \geq 0} \quad u(C_1),
\end{equation}
subject to the Period 1 budget constraint:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: period 1 bc}
    m^d_1 + p_{m,1}(D) \cdot m^f_1 = y_1 - (b_1 - D).
\end{equation}
In order for default to occur in equilibrium, the household must internalise the effect its defaulting decision has on the price of imported inputs.


\subsection{Domestic Intermediary}

A continuum of ex-ante identical domestic intermediaries $i\in[0,1]$ exist in a perfectly competitive market to intermediate imports $m^f$ in periods $t \in \{0,1\}$. These intermediaries fund the purchase of imports which they proceed to sell to the representative household. They also possess a time-variant level of net-worth $n_{i,t}$, which is centred around an exogenous long-run net-worth $\bar{n}$. \\

Each period, the intermediaries are subject to a 'cash-in-advance' constraint when purchasing foreign inputs, requiring that they hold enough interest bearing foreign assets, working capital $\kappa_{i,t}$, to cover the entirety of their import bill less their net-worth. They contract with the international lender to borrow this working capital at a gross lending rate of $Z_{i,t}$, with repayment due at the end of the same period the contract was initiated.\\


Thus, an intermediary balance sheet in period $t$ is thereby given by:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: intermediary balance sheet}
    p^f m^f_{i,t} = n_{i,t} + \kappa_{i,t}, \quad \text{for } t\in\{0,1\}.
\end{equation}

where $p^f$ is the exogenous time-invariant price of foreign inputs, defined in terms of the price of the domestic goods. Under this formulation $p^f$ can be interpreted as the real terms-of-trade.\footnote{In this small open-economy, the uncovered interest-rate parity condition implies that the domestic risk-free interest rate is identical to the world rate $R^*$.} \\

Crucially, the domestic intermediary purchases a portion of the Domestic Household's intertemporal debt to supplement its long-run equity $\bar{n}$. Thus, the intermediary's net-worth depends precisely on the period and the sovereign's default choice:
\begin{align}
    \text{Period 0:} \quad n_{i,0} &= \bar{n} + \gamma b_0 -\gamma q_0 b_1\\
    \label{eq: net worth evolution}
    \text{Period 1:} \quad n_{i,1} &= \bar{n} + \gamma (b_1 - D) 
\end{align}
where $\gamma$ is an exogenous parameter defines the proportion of the household's debt the intermediary holds. This fixed exogenous holding of domestic bonds can be interpreted as banks being subject to a regulatory liquidity mandate requiring them to hold a fraction $\gamma$ of outstanding sovereign bonds as part of their eligible liquid asset portfolio. This captures the well-documented home bias in sovereign debt holdings across emerging market banking sectors \citep{gennaioli_sovereign_2014}. The parameter $\gamma$ is therefore institutional and exogenous to the intermediary's optimisation problem.\\

Following \citet{christiano_risk_2014}, before they can deliver these inputs to the household, the domestic intermediary is subject to an idiosyncratic shock $\omega_{i,t}$, reducing or expanding the effective aggregate amount of inputs available to sell to the Domestic Household:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: intermediary effective inputs}
    M^f_t = \int^1_0 \omega_{i,t} m^f_{i,t} di,
\end{equation}
where $M^f_t$ is defined as the aggregate effective foreign inputs available for sale to the Domestic Household. This shock $\omega_{i,t}$ is drawn independently each period from a log-normal distribution $F(\omega)$ with mean 1 and variance $\sigma^2_{\omega}$.\\

Because the realised idiosyncratic shock $\omega_{i,t}$ is private information to the intermediary, the financial contract is subject to a Costly State Verification (CSV) friction \citep{bernanke_chapter_1999}. If the intermediary's realised revenue is insufficient to cover their debt obligation, they become insolvent. I define the cutoff threshold $\bar{\omega}_{i,t}$ as the exact realisation of the shock where the intermediary's revenue equals their debt repayment $Z_{i,t} \kappa_{i,t}$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: intermediary cutoff threshold}
    \bar{\omega}_{i,t} p_{m,t} m^f_{i,t} = Z_{i,t}(p^f m^f_{i,t} -  n_{i,t}).
\end{equation}

If $\omega_{i,t} \geq \bar{\omega}_{i,t}$, the intermediary repays the loan in full and retains the surplus. However, if $\omega_{i,t} < \bar{\omega}_{i,t}$, the intermediary defaults. The international lender must pay an auditing cost to observe the true realisation of $\omega_{i,t}$, destroying a fraction $\mu\in(0,1)$ of the realised assets, leaving the lender with $(1-\mu)\omega_{i,t} p_{m,t} m_{i,t}^f$. \\

As intermediaries exist in a perfectly competitive market for foreign inputs, with free entry and exits, they choose the level of inputs to supply to maximise their per-period profits:

\begin{equation} \label{eq: intermediary problem}
    \max_{m^f_{i,t}} \quad [1-\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})] p_{m,t}m^f_{i,t},
\end{equation}

which once solved yields the intermediary's optimality condition, requiring  the expected marginal surplus from scaling up supply equals the marginal cost of a higher default threshold,

\begin{equation} \label{eq: intermediary optimality condition}
    [1-\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})]p_{m,t}m^f_{i,t}=\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})Z_{i,t}n_{i,t}.
\end{equation}\\

Unlike the bilateral optimal contract in \cite{christiano_risk_2014}, intermediaries in this model take the lending rate $Z_{i,t}$ as given in a competitive credit market. This implies that intermediaries do not internalise the marginal auditing costs when choosing their scale of intermediation, generating a steeper external finance premium for a given level of net worth than is the case in the joint-contracting case. This departure can be considered to reflect the competitive and fragmented nature of the domestic banking sector.

\subsection{The International Lender}

The International Lender acts as a risk-neutral, infinitely funded lender in both the intertemporal bond market $b$ to the Domestic Household and the working capital market $\kappa$ to the Domestic Intermediary.

\subsubsection*{Working Capital Market}

Because the international lender operates in a competitive market, they must break even in expectation. The lender's participation constraint equates the expected payoff of the loan to the opportunity cost of lending at the risk-free rate $R^*$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: lender expected return integral}
    \left[ \int_{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}}^\infty \bar{\omega}_{i,t} dF(\omega) + (1-\mu) \int_0^{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}} \omega dF(\omega) \right] p_{m,t} m^f_{i,t} = R^* \kappa_{i,t}.
\end{equation}


Let $\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) \equiv \int_0^{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}} \omega dF(\omega) + \bar{\omega}_{i,t} [1 - F(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})]$ denote the expected gross share of revenue going to the lender, and $\mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) \equiv \mu \int_0^{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}} \omega dF(\omega)$ as the expected auditing costs. The zero-profit condition simplifies to:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: participation constraint}
    \left[ \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) \right] p_{m,t} m^f_{i,t} = R^* p^f m^f_{i,t} - n_{i,t}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Financial Friction}

By dividing the intermediary's threshold definition \eqref{eq: intermediary cutoff threshold} by the lender's participation constraint \eqref{eq: participation constraint}, we can isolate the relationship between intermediary net-worth and the contractually-optimal gross lending rate $Z_{i,t}$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: endogenous pm}
    Z_{i,t}=R^*\underbrace{\frac{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}}{\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})-\mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})}}_{\text{external finance premium}}.
\end{equation}

%By substituting the intermediary's balance sheet identity ($\kappa_{i,t} = $) into the participation constraint, we can isolate the domestic wholesale price of imports $p_{m,t}$ as a markup over the world price $p^f$. \\

% I assume that in Period 0, prior to any sovereign default, intermediaries are sufficiently capitalised ($n_{i,0}$ is high) such that the external finance premium is negligible ($p_{m,0} \approx p^f_0 R^*$). \\

%However, in Period 1, the sovereign's default choice $D$ directly erodes bank net worth $n_{i,1}$. The Period 1 domestic price of working capital is therefore dictated by a strictly convex external finance premium:
%\begin{equation} \label{eq: endogenous pm}
%    p_{m,1} = p^f_1 R^* \underbrace{ \left[ \frac{1 - \frac{n_{i,1}}{p^f_1 m^f_{i,1}}}{\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,1})} \right]. }_{\text{External Finance Premium}}
%\end{equation}
% From \eqref{eq: endogenous pm}, it is evident that the wedge within the domestic price of imports is strictly decreasing in the domestic intermediaries' net worth. All else equal, a greater default rate $D$ lowers $n_{i,1}$, forcing intermediaries to operate with higher leverage. Crucially, the magnitude of this wedge increases convexly with default. As net worth falls and the required repayment threshold $\bar{\omega}_{i,1}$ rises, the expected deadweight auditing costs $\mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,1})$ in the denominator accelerate. To compensate for these expected auditing losses and satisfy their participation constraint, the international lender demands an exponentially higher gross return, causing the domestic price $p_{m,1}$ to spike. \\

% This working capital friction ensures that the macroeconomic penalty of sovereign default is endogenously pro-cyclical (Mendoza and Yue, 2012). Because domestic and foreign inputs are complements in the CES aggregator ($\eta < 0$), the efficiency loss caused by an increase in $p_{m,1}$ depends on the demand for foreign inputs $m^f_1$, and thus is pro-cyclical in the aggregate state $y_1$.

Dividing the intermediary's optimal supply condition \eqref{eq: intermediary optimality condition} by the lender's participation constraint \eqref{eq: participation constraint}, and substituting in the external finance premium \eqref{eq: endogenous pm} yields the relationship between net-worth and $\bar{\omega}_{i,t}$ that pins down $\bar{\omega}_{i,t}$ as a function of the intermediary's leverage ratio:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: omega leverage}
    1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) = \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) \, \bar{\omega}_{i,t} 
    \cdot \frac{n_{i,t}}{p^fm_{i,t}^f - n_{i,t}}.
\end{equation}

Solving \eqref{eq: participation constraint} for $p_{m,t}$ illustrates how the CSV friction is passed on to the Domestic Household via the price of foreign inputs:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: pm friction}
    p_{m,t} = p^fR^* \cdot 
    \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{n_{i,t}}{p^fm_{i,t}^f}\right)}_{\text{leverage}} 
    \cdot 
    \underbrace{\frac{1}{\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})}}_{\text{CSV friction}}.
\end{equation}

I assume intermediaries are sufficiently capitalised in Period 0 to result in a negligible Period 0 external finance premium, $p_{m,0} \approx p^fR^*$.

\subsubsection*{Intertemporal Bond Market}
The Lender is also risk-neutral in its pricing of household debt. The payoff, and as such the Lender's price $q_0$, depends on the expected Period 1 post-default recovery rate:
\begin{equation}
    q_0(y_0, b_1) = \frac{1}{R^*} \mathbb{E}_0 \left[ 1 - \frac{D(y_1, b_1)}{b_1} \right].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Equilibrium and Market Clearing}

A competitive equilibrium in this economy is defined as a sequence of prices $\{q_0, p_{m,0}, p_{m,1}, \bar{\omega}_0, \bar{\omega}_1, Z_t\},$ and allocations $\{C_t, m^d_t, m^f_t, M^f_t, b_1, D, N_t, K_t\}$ such that: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item (i) the Representative Household optimises their lifetime utility $V_0$,
    \item (ii) the Domestic Intermediaries selects $m^f$, given their working-capital loan contract $Z_{i,t}$, that maximises expected returns on net-worth,
    \item (iii) the International Lender prices $b_t$ at the risk-neutral rate and its zero-profit participation constraint holds, and 
    \item (iv) the sovereign debt market ($b$), the foreign input market ($m^f$) and the domestic input market ($m^d$) all clear.
\end{itemize}

Because all domestic intermediaries $i \in [0,1]$ are ex-ante identical, they all hold the same net worth in Period 0 ($n_{i,0} = N_0$), purchase the same volume of raw imports ($m^f_{i,0} = M^f_0$), borrow the same working capital ($\kappa_{i,0} = K_0$), and face the same default threshold ($\bar{\omega}_{i,0} = \bar{\omega}_0$). 

\subsubsection*{Sovereign Debt Market}
The aggregate sovereign debt issued in Period 0, $B_1$, must equal the total amount demanded by the domestic intermediaries and the international lender. The domestic banking sector demands a fixed fraction $\gamma$, leaving the International Lender to hold the remainder:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: bond market clearing}
    B_1 = \gamma B_1 + (1-\gamma) B_1 = b_1.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Foreign Inputs Market}

The total effective foreign inputs available for the domestic household to use in production ($m^f_t$) is equal to the aggregate raw imports purchased by the intermediaries ($M^f_t$) minus the goods destroyed by the international lender's auditing process during intermediary bankruptcies:
\begin{equation} \label{eq: import market clearing}
    m^f_t = M^f_t \left( 1 - \mu \int_0^{\bar{\omega}_t} \omega dF(\omega) \right) = M^f_t \big[ 1 - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_t) \big].
\end{equation}

The internal price of foreign inputs $p_{m,t}$ clears the market via the household's demand function equaling the intermediary supply, net of auditing losses \eqref{eq: import market clearing}.

\subsubsection*{Aggregate Resource Constraints}
In Period 0, the economy issues debt to the RoW to finance excess consumption and raw imports:
\begin{equation}
    m_0^d + R^* p^f M_0^f - (R^* - 1)N_0 = y_0 + \bar{n} - (1-\gamma)(b_0 - q_0 b_1)
    \label{eq:arc-p0}
\end{equation}

In Period 1, the economy must service its externally held debt out of its domestic endowment, while funding its new intra-period raw imports. The Period 1 Balance of Payments is:
\begin{equation}
    m_1^d + R^* p^f M_1^f - (R^* - 1)N_1 = y_1 + \bar{n} - (1-\gamma)(b_1 - D)
    \label{eq:arc-p1}
\end{equation}

\section{Optimisation Policies}

\subsection{Domestic Household}

\subsubsection*{Period 1}

Setting up the Domestic Household's Period 1 Lagrangian,
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_1 = u(C_1) + \lambda_1 \left[ y_1 - (b_1 - D) - m_1^d - p_{m,1}(D) m_1^f \right],
    \label{eq:lagrangian-p1}
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda_1$ denotes the Period 1 Lagrange multiplier for the household's budget constraint.

\paragraph{Intratemporal Optimality: }
The first-order conditions with respect to $m_1^d$ and $m_1^f$ yield
%
\begin{align}
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_1}{\partial m_t^d} &= u'(C_t) \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial m_t^d} - \lambda_t = 0, 
    \label{eq:foc-md} \\[6pt]
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_1}{\partial m_t^f} &= u'(C_t) \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial m_t^f} - \lambda_t p_{m,t} = 0.
    \label{eq:foc-mf}
\end{align}
%
From the CES aggregator \eqref{eq: CES aggregator}, the marginal products are
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial m_t^d} = \alpha \left( \frac{C_t}{m_t^d} \right)^{1-\eta}, \qquad
    \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial m_t^f} = (1-\alpha) \left( \frac{C_t}{m_t^f} \right)^{1-\eta}.
\end{equation}
%
Dividing \eqref{eq:foc-mf} by \eqref{eq:foc-md}
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha} \left( \frac{m_t^d}{m_t^f} \right)^{1-\eta} = p_{m,t},
\end{equation}
%
which, solving for $m_t^f$, gives the standard intratemporal demand function for foreign inputs:
%
\begin{equation}
    m_t^f = m_t^d \left( \frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha \, p_{m,t}} \right)^{\frac{1}{1-\eta}}.
    \label{eq:demand-mf}
\end{equation}
%
Note also that the shadow value of income can be written as
%
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_t = u'(C_t) \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial m_t^d}, \quad \text{for } t \in \{0,1\}.
    \label{eq:lambda}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Optimal Default: }
The household's first-order condition with respect to $D \in [0, b_1]$ is
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_1}{\partial D} = \lambda_1 \left[ 1 - m_1^f \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} \right].
\end{equation}
%
The marginal benefit of an additional unit of default is the relief to the budget constraint of one unit of numéraire. The marginal cost is the induced increase in the domestic price of foreign inputs, weighted by the quantity of imports demanded. The full KKT conditions, incorporating both bounds on $D$, are
%
\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        D = 0 
            & \text{if } \; 1 \leq m_1^f 
              \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}\bigg|_{D=0}
            \hfill \text{(no default),} \\[12pt]
        1 = m_1^f \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} 
            & \text{if } \; D \in (0,\, b_1)
            \hfill \text{(partial default),} \\[12pt]
        D = b_1 
            & \text{if } \; 1 \geq m_1^f 
              \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}\bigg|_{D=b_1}
            \hfill \text{(full default).}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:default-kkt}
\end{equation}
%
At an interior solution (partial default), the household equates the marginal budgetary gain from one extra unit of default with the marginal cost through the external finance premium.

\paragraph{Transmission of Default to Import Prices: }
From the intermediary's net-worth equation \eqref{eq: net worth evolution}:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} = -\gamma \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial n_{i,1}}.
    \label{eq:dpm-dD}
\end{equation}
%
An increase in $D$ erodes intermediary net worth $n_{i,1}$, which raises intermediary leverage. Higher leverage pushes up the default threshold $\bar{\omega}_{i,1}$, which in turn increases the external finance premium $Z_{i,1}/R^*$ via equation \eqref{eq: endogenous pm}. Competitive intermediaries pass this higher lending cost through to the domestic price of imports $p_{m,1}$:
%
\begin{equation*}
    D \uparrow \;\;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq: net worth evolution}}\;\; n_{i,1} \downarrow \;\;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq: omega leverage}}\;\; \bar{\omega}_{i,1} \uparrow \;\;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq: endogenous pm}}\;\; \frac{Z_{i,1}}{R^*} \uparrow \;\;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq: pm friction}}\;\; p_{m,1} \uparrow \;\;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:demand-mf}}\;\; m_1^f \downarrow \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; C_1 \downarrow
\end{equation*}

To derive $\partial p_{m,1} / \partial n_{i,1}$, differentiate the price equation \eqref{eq: pm friction} with respect to $n_{i,1}$, holding $m_{i,1}^f$ fixed:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial n_{i,1}} = p^f R^* \left( 
    \frac{
        -\dfrac{1}{p^f m_{i,1}^f} \left[ \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) \right] 
        - \left( 1 - \dfrac{n_{i,1}}{p^f m_{i,1}^f} \right) 
          \left[ \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) - \mu G'(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) \right] 
          \dfrac{\partial \bar{\omega}_{i,1}}{\partial n_{i,1}}
    }{
        \left[ \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) \right]^2
    }
    \right).
    \label{eq:dpm-dn}
\end{equation}
%
An increase in net worth reduces the domestic import price. The first term in the numerator captures the direct reduction in leverage. The second term captures the indirect effect through the lowering of $\bar{\omega}_{i,1}$, which reduces expected auditing costs and thereby compresses the CSV friction. The denominator $[\Gamma - \mu G]^2$ is the source of the convexity in the default penalty the household is subject to - as net worth falls in default and $\bar{\omega}$ rises, $\Gamma - \mu G$ shrinks, causing the denominator to contract and the magnitude of $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial n_{i,1}$ to accelerate.

\paragraph{Derivation of $\partial \bar{\omega}_{i,1} / \partial n_{i,1}$: }
The threshold $\bar{\omega}_{i,1}$ is pinned down by equation \eqref{eq: omega leverage}, which determines $\bar{\omega}$ as a function of the leverage ratio:
%
\begin{equation*}
    1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) = \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) \, \bar{\omega}_{i,t} \cdot \frac{n_{i,t}}{p^f m_{i,t}^f - n_{i,t}}.
\end{equation*}
%
Define $\phi \equiv n_{i,t} / (p^f m_{i,t}^f - n_{i,t})$ as the equity-to-debt ratio. Implicitly differentiating with respect to $n_{i,t}$, holding $m_{i,t}^f$ fixed:
%
\begin{equation}
    -\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}}{\partial n} 
    = \left[ \Gamma''(\bar{\omega}) \, \bar{\omega} + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) \right] 
      \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}}{\partial n} \cdot \phi 
    + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) \, \bar{\omega} \cdot \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n},
    \label{eq:domega-dn-step1}
\end{equation}
%
where:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n_{i,t}} = \frac{p^f m_{i,t}^f}{(p^f m_{i,t}^f - n_{i,t})^2} > 0.
\end{equation}
%
Collecting terms on $\partial \bar{\omega} / \partial n$:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_{i,t}}{\partial n_{i,t}} 
    = \frac{
         \dfrac{-\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) \, \bar{\omega} \cdot p^f m_{i,t}^f}{(p^f m_{i,t}^f - n_{i,t})^2}
    }{
        \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) + \left[ \Gamma''(\bar{\omega}) \, \bar{\omega} + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) \right] \phi
    },
    \label{eq:domega-dn}
\end{equation}
%
which is always negative. A higher net worth therefore lowers the default threshold. The numerator is negative because $\Gamma' > 0$ and $\bar{\omega} > 0$. The denominator is positive provided the second-order conditions of the intermediary's problem are satisfied.\footnote{Specifically, the denominator's sign requires $\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) + [\Gamma''(\bar{\omega})\bar{\omega} + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega})]\phi > 0$, which holds when the intermediary's profit function is concave in $m_{i,t}^f$ at the optimum.} \\

Crucially, as leverage increases ($n_{i,t}$ falls relative to $p^f m_{i,t}^f$), the denominator contracts, while the numerator grows in magnitude through the $(p^f m^f - n)^2$ term. This means $|\partial \bar{\omega} / \partial n|$ is increasing in leverage, meaning the sensitivity of the default threshold to net worth accelerates as intermediaries become more leveraged. Combined with the shrinking denominator $[\Gamma - \mu G]^2$ in equation \eqref{eq:dpm-dn}, this generates the convexity of the external finance premium: a linear increase in sovereign default triggers a more-than-proportional increase in the domestic import price $p_{m,1}$.

\paragraph{Log-Normal Derivatives: }
Given $\omega \sim LN(-\sigma_\omega^2/2, \, \sigma_\omega^2)$, the derivatives of $\Gamma$ and $G$ take closed-form expressions:
%
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) = 1 - \Phi\!\left( \frac{\ln(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) + \sigma_\omega^2/2}{\sigma_\omega} \right), \qquad
    \mu G'(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) = \frac{\mu}{\sigma_\omega} \varphi\!\left( \frac{\ln(\bar{\omega}_{i,1}) + \sigma_\omega^2/2}{\sigma_\omega} \right),
    \label{eq:lognormal-derivs}
\end{equation}
%
where $\Phi$ and $\varphi$ denote the standard-normal CDF and PDF, respectively. 

% As $\bar{\omega}$ rises into the right tail of the log-normal distribution, $\Gamma'(\bar{\omega})$ falls (the lender's marginal gain from raising the threshold diminishes) while $\mu G'(\bar{\omega})$ peaks and declines. The net effect is that $\Gamma' - \mu G'$ decreases monotonically over the relevant range, amplifying $|\partial \bar{\omega}/\partial n|$ and thereby the convexity of $p_{m,1}$ in default.


\subsubsection*{Period 0}

In Period 0, the household takes their Period 1 default decision as given when determining the optimal issuance of intertemporal bonds, selecting optimal allocations of $m_0^f$, $m_0^d$ and $b_1$, whilst acting as a price-taker in their own debt issuance. The household's Period 0 Lagrangian is:
%
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_0 = u(C_0) + \beta \mathbb{E}_0[V_1(y_1, b_1)] + \lambda_0 \left[ y_0 - b_0 + q_0 b_1 - m_0^d - p_{m,0} m_0^f \right],
    \label{eq:lagrangian-p0}
\end{equation}
%
where $V_1(y_1, b_1)$ is the household's optimised Period 1 value function. \\

The intratemporal first-order conditions with respect to $m_0^d$ and $m_0^f$ are identical in structure to the Period 1 conditions, yielding the same demand function \eqref{eq:demand-mf} evaluated at Period 0 prices.

\paragraph{Euler Equation: }
The first-order condition with respect to $b_1$ yields the intertemporal Euler equation. Using the envelope theorem, the marginal value of an additional unit of debt carried into Period 1 is:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial V_1}{\partial b_1} = \lambda_1 \left[ -1 - m_1^f \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial b_1} \right],
\end{equation}
%
where the first term is the direct repayment burden (net of the marginal default response) and the second captures the indirect effect of higher debt on import prices through intermediary net worth. Since $n_{i,1} = \bar{n} + \gamma(b_1 - D)$, and the household takes $D$ as given at the time of issuance:
%
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial b_1} = \gamma \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial n_{i,1}}.
\end{equation}
%
It is worth noting an increase in $b_1$ raises intermediary net worth, since intermediaries hold $\gamma b_1$ in sovereign bonds, and more issuance means a larger asset for the bank in Period 1, conditional on a given default level $D$. \\

Combining with the Period 0 first-order condition:
%
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_0 q_0 = \beta \mathbb{E}_0 \left[ \lambda_1 \left( 1 + \gamma m_1^f \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial n_{i,1}} \right) \right].
    \label{eq:euler}
\end{equation}
%
The left-hand side is the marginal utility cost of purchasing one unit of debt at price $q_0$ in Period 0. The right-hand side is the discounted expected marginal benefit, which comprises two components: the direct repayment of one unit of resources, and the improvement in Period 1 import prices from the additional net worth that $\gamma$ units of bond holdings deliver to intermediaries. \\

As $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial n_{i,1} < 0$, higher debt issuance, while strengthening bank balance sheets, also increases the household's future repayment burden. In equilibrium, the household balances the Period 0 consumption benefit of additional borrowing against both the direct repayment cost and the indirect effect on import prices.

%\paragraph{Remark on Price-Taking.}
%The Euler equation \eqref{eq:euler} is derived under the assumption that the household takes $q_0$ as parametric --- that is, they do not internalise the effect of their issuance decision on the bond price schedule $q_0(y_0, b_1)$. This is in contrast to the standard Eaton-Gersovitz framework, where the sovereign internalises the bond price schedule and the Euler equation would include an additional term $b_1 \, \partial q_0 / \partial b_1$ on the left-hand side. The price-taking assumption here can be interpreted as a coordination failure in the issuance of new debt \citep{goodhart2018}, where the sovereign does not account for the impact of marginal borrowing on its risk premium. The equilibrium bond price \eqref{eq:bond-price} therefore holds as a fixed-point condition rather than a constraint the household optimises over.
