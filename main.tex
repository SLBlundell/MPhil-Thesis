\section{The Model Environment}
%========================================================================

I present an endowment economy inhabited by three distinct agents: a representative household (the sovereign), a continuum of risk-neutral domestic import intermediaries (domestic banks) indexed by $i \in [0,1]$, and a risk-neutral international lender. The discrete-time model spans two periods, $t \in \{0,1\}$.

\subsection{The Economy}

The representative household has time-separable expected utility over consumption:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:V0}
V_0 = u(C_0) + \beta \, \E_0\bigl[u(C_1)\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $u$ is strictly increasing, strictly concave, and satisfies Inada conditions, and $\beta \in (0,1)$ is the subjective discount factor.

The household produces the final consumption good $C_t$ from domestic inputs $\md_t$ and foreign inputs $\mf_t$ via a CES aggregator:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:CES}
C_t = \bigl[\alpha (\md_t)^{\eta} + (1-\alpha)(\mf_t)^{\eta}\bigr]^{1/\eta}, \qquad t \in \{0,1\},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is the Armington weight on domestic inputs and $\eta < 0$ governs the elasticity of substitution $\sigma = 1/(1-\eta) \in (0,1)$. The restriction $\eta < 0$ ensures the two inputs are strict gross complements: the household cannot substitute away from foreign inputs to avoid punitive price increases.

The household receives a per-period endowment $y_t$ of domestic inputs. Period 1 endowment growth $g_1 \equiv y_1/y_0$ follows an AR(1) process in logs:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:endowgrowth}
\log(g_1) = (1-\rho)\log(\mu_g) + \rho \log(g_0) + \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_g^2).
\end{equation}

There exists an exogenous risk-free gross world interest rate $R^*>1$. Following \citet{eaton_debt_1981}, the international lender has access to a frictionless world capital market at this rate. As such, the lender will only participate in lending activities, both interperiod and intraperiod, which guarantee them a rate of return at least as high as this market rate.

\subsection{Nominal Structure and Exchange Rate} \label{sec:nominal}
Let $P_t$ denote the domestic-currency price of domestic goods, and $e_t$ the exchange rate, expressed as units of domestic currency per unit of foreign currency. The real exchange rate is defined as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:real-exch}
    RER_t \equiv \frac{e_t}{P_t},
\end{equation}
where foreign input prices are normalised to one. I assume that bonds within the domestic economy are issued in real-terms, representing a fixed quantity of domestic output, as to ensure the real net-of-default payoff for the household is invariant to domestic price levels. \\

Domestic input purchases are subject to a cash-in-advance constraint that pins down domestic price levels:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:cash-in-advance}
    MS_t=P_t \cdot m^d_t,
\end{equation}
where $MS_t$ denotes the domestic money supply. \\

Proposition \ref{prop:neutrality} below establishes that, given neutrality assumptions, the real exchange rate reduces to $\bar{RER}=1$, with one unit of foreign input exchanging for one unit of domestic input on the (frictionless) world market. The remainder of the analysis proceeds in real terms.

\subsection{Financial Instruments and the CSV Friction}\label{sec:csv}

The household issues one-period, non-contingent bonds $b_1$ at price $q_0$ in Period 0. Each bond promises one unit of the domestic num\'{e}raire at maturity. A fraction $\gamma \in (0,1)$ of outstanding bonds is held by the domestic intermediary sector; the remainder $(1-\gamma)$ is held by the international lender. The parameter $\gamma$ is exogenous and institutional, reflecting a regulatory liquidity mandate requiring domestic banks to hold a fraction of outstanding sovereign bonds as eligible liquid assets. This captures the well-documented home bias in sovereign debt holdings across emerging market banking sectors \citep{gennaioli_sovereign_2014}.

Upon realisation of the Period 1 endowment, the sovereign household chooses a continuous partial default $D \in [0, b_1]$, interpreted as a haircut on outstanding debt. As an important departure from \citet{arellano_default_2008}, the household does not internalise the effect of its default decision on the price of debt. Rather, default occurs in equilibrium as the household internalises the impact of default on import prices. The sovereign in this setting can be thought of as being subject to a coordination failure in the issuance of new debt \citep{goodhart_debt_2018}.

Following the structure proposed by \citet{christiano_risk_2014}, each intermediary is subject to an idiosyncratic productivity shock $\omega_{i,t}$, drawn independently each period from a log-normal distribution with mean one and variance $\sigma_\omega^2$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:omega_dist}
\log(\omega_{i,t}) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(-\tfrac{\sigma_\omega^2}{2},\, \sigma_\omega^2\right).
\end{equation}
Let $F(\omega)$ and $f(\omega)$ denote the CDF and PDF of this distribution. Because the realised shock is private information to the intermediary, the financial contract between the intermediary and the international lender is subject to a costly state verification (CSV) friction, following \citet{bernanke_chapter_1999}.

The CSV friction is characterised by two functions of the default threshold $\bar{\omega} \geq 0$ that appear throughout the analysis. The expected gross share of intermediary revenue accruing to the lender under the optimal debt contract is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gammadef}
\Gamma(\bar{\omega}) \equiv \int_0^{\bar{\omega}} \omega \, dF(\omega) + \bar{\omega}\bigl[1 - F(\bar{\omega})\bigr],
\end{equation}
and the expected revenue share from defaulting intermediaries is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Gdef}
G(\bar{\omega}) \equiv \int_0^{\bar{\omega}} \omega \, dF(\omega),
\end{equation}
so that $\mu G(\bar{\omega})$ is the expected deadweight auditing cost, where $\mu \in (0,1)$ is the monitoring cost parameter. These functions satisfy $\Gamma(0) = 0$, $\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) = 1 - F(\bar{\omega}) > 0$, and $G'(\bar{\omega}) = \bar{\omega} f(\bar{\omega}) \geq 0$. Under the log-normal specification \eqref{eq:omega_dist}, the derivatives admit the closed forms:
\begin{align}
\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}) &= 1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{\ln(\bar{\omega}) + \sigma_\omega^2/2}{\sigma_\omega}\right), \label{eq:Gammaprime}\\[4pt]
\mu G'(\bar{\omega}) &= \frac{\mu}{\sigma_\omega}\,\varphi\!\left(\frac{\ln(\bar{\omega}) + \sigma_\omega^2/2}{\sigma_\omega}\right), \label{eq:muGprime} \\
\Gamma''(\bar{\omega}) &= -\frac{1}{\bar{\omega}\,\sigma_\omega}\,
\varphi\!\left(\frac{\ln\bar{\omega}
+ \sigma_\omega^2/2}{\sigma_\omega}\right), \label{eq:Gammapp}
\end{align}
where $\Phi$ and $\varphi$ denote the standard normal CDF and PDF. It is worth noting that $\Gamma''$ is strictly negative for all $\bar{\omega} > 0$.

\subsection{Sequence of Events} \label{sec:timings}

The within-period timing is as follows.

\paragraph{Period 0:}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item The household receives endowment $y_0$.
\item The household issues bonds $b_1$ at price $q_0$; fraction $\gamma$ purchased by intermediaries, remainder by the international lender.
\item Intermediaries borrow working capital $\kappa_0$ from the international lender and intermediate foreign inputs $\mf_0$.
\item Idiosyncratic shocks $\omega_{i,0}$ are realised; solvent intermediaries deliver inputs and distribute surplus $\Pi_0$ to the household.
\item The household produces and consumes $C_0$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Period 1:}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item The aggregate endowment shock $g_1$ is realised, determining $y_1 = g_1 y_0$.
\item The household chooses partial default $D \in [0, b_1]$, the Intermediaries' net worth $n_1$ is updated.
\item Intermediaries borrow working capital $\kappa_1$ subject to the CSV contract, determining the domestic price of foreign inputs $p_{m,1}$.
\item Idiosyncratic shocks $\omega_{i,1}$ are realised; solvent intermediaries deliver inputs and distribute surplus $\Pi_1$ to the household.
\item The household produces and consumes $C_1$.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Agents}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Domestic Household}

The representative household acts as both a consumer and a final-goods producer, aggregating domestic and foreign inputs via \eqref{eq:CES}. It takes the internal price of foreign inputs, $p_{m,t}$ - the relative price expressed in units of domestic inputs - as given in each period.

\paragraph{Period 0.} The household chooses domestic inputs $\md_0$, foreign inputs $\mf_0$, and bond issuance $b_1$ to maximise \eqref{eq:V0} subject to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bc0}
\md_0 + p_{m,0}\,\mf_0 = y_0 - b_0 + q_0 b_1 + \Pi_0,
\end{equation}
where $b_0$ denotes pre-existing debt obligations and $\Pi_t$ is a lump-sum dividend received from the domestic intermediary sector, defined below. The household acts as a price-taker in its own debt issuance.

\paragraph{Period 1.} After $y_1$ is realised, the household chooses default $D \in [0, b_1]$ and inputs $(\md_1, \mf_1)$ to solve:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hh_p1}
\max_{D \in [0,b_1],\; \md_1 \geq 0,\; \mf_1 \geq 0} \; u(C_1),
\end{equation}
subject to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bc1}
\md_1 + p_{m,1}(D) \cdot \mf_1 = y_1 - (b_1 - D) + \Pi_1.
\end{equation}
The import price $p_{m,1}$ depends on $D$ through the intermediary balance sheet channel described below. In order for default to occur in equilibrium, the household must internalise this effect. Crucially, while the household internalises the dependence of $p_{m,1}$ on $D$, it treats the dividends $\Pi_0, \Pi_1$ as lump sums, that is, it does not internalise $\partial \Pi_t / \partial D$ when choosing default. This asymmetry reflects the fact that import prices are directly observable and contractually relevant to the household's purchasing decisions, whereas the dividend is a residual claim on aggregate intermediary surplus that the atomistic households do not perceive to depend on their own actions.

\subsection{Domestic Intermediary}\label{sec:intermediary}

A continuum of ex-ante identical domestic intermediaries $i \in [0,1]$ operate in a perfectly competitive market with free entry and exit to intermediate foreign inputs $\mf$ in each period. Each intermediary purchases imports using a combination of its own net worth $n_{i,t}$ and working capital $\kappa_{i,t}$ borrowed from the international lender at gross intra-period rate $Z_{i,t}$, with repayment due within the same period. The intermediary's intra-period balance sheet is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:balsheet}
 \mf_{i,t} = n_{i,t} + \kappa_{i,t}, \qquad t \in \{0,1\}.
\end{equation}
It is worth emphasising that $\kappa_{i,t}$ is an intra-period contract---borrowing and repayment occur within the same period $t$---distinct from the inter-period sovereign bond $b_1$ issued in Period 0 and maturing in Period 1. The international lender's opportunity cost is $\Rstar$ for both contracts, but the two markets clear independently.

The intermediary's net worth is centred around an exogenous long-run level $\bar{n} > 0$ and depends on the period and the sovereign's default choice:
\begin{align}
n_{i,0} &= \bar{n} + \gamma b_0 - \gamma q_0 b_1, \label{eq:nw0}\\
n_{i,1} &= \bar{n} + \gamma(b_1 - D). \label{eq:nw1}
\end{align}

Before delivering inputs to the household, intermediary $i$ is subject to the idiosyncratic shock $\omega_{i,t}$, so that aggregate effective foreign inputs available for sale are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:agg_supply}
\Mf_t = \int_0^1 \omega_{i,t}\,\mf_{i,t}\,di.
\end{equation}

The cutoff threshold $\bar{\omega}_{i,t}$ is the exact shock realisation at which the intermediary just breaks even:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:threshold}
\bar{\omega}_{i,t}\, p_{m,t}\, \mf_{i,t} = Z_{i,t}\,\kappa_{i,t} = Z_{i,t}\bigl( \mf_{i,t} - n_{i,t}\bigr).
\end{equation}
If $\omega_{i,t} \geq \bar{\omega}_{i,t}$, the intermediary repays in full and retains the surplus. If $\omega_{i,t} < \bar{\omega}_{i,t}$, the intermediary defaults; the lender pays the auditing cost, destroying a fraction $\mu$ of realised assets and recovering $(1-\mu)\omega_{i,t}\,p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t}$.

Each intermediary chooses $\mf_{i,t}$ to maximise expected profits:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:int_obj}
\max_{\mf_{i,t}} \; \bigl[1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})\bigr]\,p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t},
\end{equation}
yielding the first-order condition:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:int_foc}
\bigl[1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})\bigr]\,p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t} = \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})\,Z_{i,t}\,n_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
Unlike the bilateral optimal contract in \citet{christiano_risk_2014}, intermediaries here take $Z_{i,t}$ as given in a competitive credit market. They do not internalise the marginal auditing costs when choosing their scale of intermediation, generating a steeper external finance premium for a given level of net worth. This departure reflects the competitive and fragmented nature of the domestic banking sector.

\paragraph{Intermediary dividends.} All intermediaries are owned by the representative household, who acts as the residual claimant on any surplus they generate. Once the idiosyncratic shock is realised and obligations to the international lender are settled, solvent intermediaries distribute their surplus revenue as a lump-sum dividend. The per-unit surplus of intermediary $i$, net of working-capital repayment, is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pi_i}
\pi_{i,t} = \begin{cases} (\omega_{i,t} - \bar{\omega}_t)\,p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t} & \text{if } \omega_{i,t} \geq \bar{\omega}_t, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Aggregating across the continuum of intermediaries and applying the law of large numbers:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Pi_agg}
\Pi_t = \int_{\bar{\omega}_t}^{\infty} (\omega - \bar{\omega}_t)\,p_{m,t}\,\Mf_t\,dF(\omega) = \bigl[1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_t)\bigr]\,p_{m,t}\,\Mf_t.
\end{equation}
This is the aggregate dividend $\Pi_t$ that enters the household's budget constraints \eqref{eq:bc0}--\eqref{eq:bc1}.

\subsection{International Lender}

The international lender is risk-neutral, infinitely funded, and prices all contracts at the risk-free rate $\Rstar$.

\paragraph{Working capital market.} The lender's zero-profit participation constraint equates the expected payoff to the opportunity cost:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lender_zp}
\left[\int_{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}}^{\infty} \bar{\omega}_{i,t}\,dF(\omega) + (1-\mu)\!\int_0^{\bar{\omega}_{i,t}} \omega\,dF(\omega)\right] p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t} = \Rstar \kappa_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
Using the CSV functions from \Cref{sec:csv}, this simplifies to:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lender_zp_compact}
\bigl[\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_{i,t}) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_{i,t})\bigr]\,p_{m,t}\,\mf_{i,t} = \Rstar\bigl( \mf_{i,t} - n_{i,t}\bigr).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Intertemporal bond market.} The lender prices household debt at the risk-neutral expected recovery rate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bondprice}
q_0(y_0, b_1) = \frac{1}{\Rstar}\,\E_0\!\left[1 - \frac{D(y_1, b_1)}{b_1}\right].
\end{equation}

\section{Equilibrium Definitions}
%========================================================================

\subsection{Aggregation and Market Clearing}

Because all intermediaries are ex-ante identical, in any symmetric equilibrium they hold the same net worth ($n_{i,t} = N_t$), purchase the same raw imports ($\mf_{i,t} = \Mf_t$), borrow the same working capital ($\kappa_{i,t} = K_t$), and face the same default threshold ($\bar{\omega}_{i,t} = \bar{\omega}_t$). Henceforth all intermediary-level variables are written without the $i$ subscript.

Three markets must clear in equilibrium. The sovereign debt market requires that total issuance equals total demand:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mc_debt}
b_1 = \gamma b_1 + (1-\gamma)b_1.
\end{equation}
The foreign inputs market requires that the household's demand for effective foreign inputs equals aggregate intermediary supply net of auditing losses:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mc_foreign}
\mf_t = \Mf_t\bigl[1 - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_t)\bigr].
\end{equation}
The domestic inputs market clears by Walras' law given the first two conditions and the household budget constraint.

Aggregating across agents and imposing market clearing, the economy's balance-of-payments constraints are:
\begin{align}
\md_0 + \Rstar  \Mf_0 - (\Rstar - 1)N_0 &= y_0 + \bar{n} - (1-\gamma)(b_0 - q_0 b_1), \label{eq:bop0}\\
\md_1 + \Rstar  \Mf_1 - (\Rstar - 1)N_1 &= y_1 + \bar{n} - (1-\gamma)(b_1 - D). \label{eq:bop1}
\end{align}

\subsection{Equilibrium Definition}

\begin{definition}[Competitive Equilibrium]\label{def:equil}
Given initial debt $b_0$, long-run net worth $\bar{n}$, exogenous global parameter $R^*$ and realised aggregate endowments $(y_0, y_1)$, a competitive equilibrium is defined as a collection of prices $\{q_0,\, p_{m,0},\, p_{m,1},\, Z_0,\, Z_1\}$, thresholds $\{\bar{\omega}_0,\, \bar{\omega}_1\}$, and allocations $\{C_t,\, \md_t,\, \mf_t,\, \Mf_t,\, b_1,\, D,\, N_t,\, K_t,\, \Pi_t\}_{t=0,1}$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item The representative household optimises lifetime utility, choosing $(\md_t, \mf_t, b_1, D)$ given prices $(q_0, p_{m,0}, p_{m,1})$;
\item Each domestic intermediary selects $\mf_{i,t}$ to maximise expected profit \eqref{eq:int_obj}, given the lending rate $Z_t$;
\item The international lender prices sovereign bonds via \eqref{eq:bondprice} and the working capital zero-profit condition \eqref{eq:lender_zp_compact} holds;
\item The sovereign debt, foreign input, and domestic input markets clear;
\item Net worth satisfies \eqref{eq:nw0}--\eqref{eq:nw1}, working capital is consistent: $K_t =  \Mf_t - N_t$, and aggregate dividends satisfy \eqref{eq:Pi_agg}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Nominal Neutrality}

Under the nominal structure defined in Section \ref{sec:nominal}, the following result establishes that the real equilibrium is self-contained:

\begin{lemma}[Nominal Neutrality]\label{prop:neutrality}
Suppose that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item sovereign bonds are denominated in units of the domestic good, so that the real payoff $(b_1 - D)$ is independent of $P_t$;
    \item domestic goods prices are fully flexible; and
    \item the real exchange rate is constant: $RER_t = \bar{RER}$ for all $t$.
\end{enumerate}
Then the real allocation $\{C_t,\, m^d_t,\, m^f_t,\, M^f_t,\, b_1,\, D,\, \bar{\omega}_t,\, Z_t,\, \Pi_t\}_{t=0,1}$ and the real prices \\
$\{q_0,\, p_{m,0},\, p_{m,1}\}$ are determined by the system of equations \emph{\eqref{eq:CES}}--\emph{\eqref{eq:euler}}  independently of the domestic money supply $MS_t$, the nominal exchange rate $e_t$, and the domestic price level $P_t$. The nominal variables are determined residually by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:price-level}
    P_t = \frac{MS_t}{m^d_t}, \qquad e_t = P_t \cdot \bar{RER},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{RER} = 1$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Express each agent's budget constraint and optimality condition in nominal terms, then deflate by $P_t$. The household's Period 1 nominal budget
constraint is
\begin{equation*}
    P_1 \cdot m^d_1 + P^{\text{nom}}_{m,1} \cdot m^f_1
    = P_1 \cdot y_1 - P_1 \cdot (b_1 - D) + P_1 \cdot \Pi_1,
\end{equation*}
where $P^{\text{nom}}_{m,1}$ is the nominal domestic-currency price of foreign inputs. Dividing by $P_1$ and letting $p_{m,1} \equiv P^{\text{nom}}_{m,1}/P_1$ recovers \eqref{eq:bc1}. The relative price $p_{m,t}$ thus captures the full real cost of foreign inputs to the domestic household, inclusive of both the underlying terms of trade and the friction-based markup, detailed in Section \ref{sec:csv}. 

The same exercise can be conducted for Period 0 equilibrium, recovering the stated real system by deflation as above.

The intermediary balance sheet in nominal terms is $e_t \cdot m^f_{i,t} = P_t \cdot n_{i,t} + e_t \cdot \kappa_{i,t}$. Dividing by $P_t$ and noting $e_t/P_t = 1$ under assumption (iii) yields \eqref{eq:balsheet}.

The CSV functions $\Gamma(\bar{\omega})$ and $G(\bar{\omega})$ are unit-free ratios, so the threshold condition \eqref{eq:threshold}, the lender's zero-profit condition \eqref{eq:lender_zp}, and the import price equation \eqref{eq:pm} are invariant to the choice of num\'{e}raire.

Since every equilibrium condition in the real system is recovered by deflation, the real allocation is independent of $MS_t$. Given the equilibrium values of $m^d_t$, the price level follows from (\ref{eq:cash-in-advance}) and the nominal exchange rate from (\ref{eq:price-level}).

\end{proof}

Let $i_t$ denote the domestic nominal interest rate. Under satisfaction of the uncovered interest rate parity condition, the nominal rate can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
    (1+i_t)=R^*\E_t\left[\frac{e_{t+1}}{e_t}\right].
\end{equation}

Under the assumption of nominal neutrality, with domestic prices being pinned down by \eqref{eq:cash-in-advance}, this becomes
\begin{equation}
    (1+i_t)=R^*\E_t\left[\frac{MS_{t+1}/m^d_{t+1}}{MS_t/m^d_t}\right].
\end{equation}
The domestic nominal rate therefore depends on monetary policy and the real allocation of domestic inputs, but is determined residually and does not feed back into the real equilibrium.

Following the Fisher equation, the domestic real interest rate $R_t$ is expressed as
\begin{equation}
    R_t=(1+i_t)\frac{P_t}{\E_t[P_{t+1}]}=R^*,
\end{equation}
with the domestic rate equalising each period to the time-invariant world gross rate $R^*$.

\section{Optimality Conditions}
%========================================================================

\subsection{The Financial Friction}

Three key relationships emerge from the intermediary and lender optimality conditions. Aggregating across intermediaries and dividing the threshold condition \eqref{eq:threshold} by the zero-profit condition \eqref{eq:lender_zp_compact}, the equilibrium lending rate satisfies:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:efp}
Z_t = \Rstar \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\bar{\omega}_t}{\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_t) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_t)}}_{\text{external finance premium}}.
\end{equation}
The ratio $Z_t / \Rstar$ exceeds unity whenever $\bar{\omega}_t > 0$ and is increasing in $\bar{\omega}_t$.

Dividing the intermediary FOC \eqref{eq:int_foc} by the zero-profit condition \eqref{eq:lender_zp_compact} and substituting \eqref{eq:efp}, the threshold $\bar{\omega}_t$ is pinned down by the leverage ratio alone:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:omega_leverage}
1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_t) = \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_t)\,\bar{\omega}_t \cdot \frac{N_t}{ \Mf_t - N_t}.
\end{equation}
For given $\Mf_t$, a decrease in $N_t$ raises leverage and increases $\bar{\omega}_t$.

Solving the zero-profit condition \eqref{eq:lender_zp_compact} for $p_{m,t}$ yields the import price equation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pm}
p_{m,t} =  \Rstar \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - \frac{N_t}{ M^f_t}\right)}_{\text{leverage}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_t) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_t)}}_{\text{CSV friction}}.
\end{equation}
I assume intermediaries are sufficiently well capitalised in Period 0 that the external finance premium is negligible, so $p_{m,0} \approx  \Rstar$.

\subsection{Household Intratemporal Optimality}

The first-order conditions with respect to $\md_t$ and $\mf_t$ from the CES aggregator \eqref{eq:CES} yield the standard intratemporal demand function:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mf_demand}
\mf_t = \md_t \left(\frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha\, p_{m,t}}\right)^{\!\frac{1}{1-\eta}},
\end{equation}
and the shadow value of income:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shadow}
\lambda_t = u'(C_t)\,\frac{\partial C_t}{\partial \md_t} = u'(C_t)\,\alpha\!\left(\frac{C_t}{\md_t}\right)^{\!1-\eta}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Optimal Default}

\begin{lemma}[Optimal Default Policy]\label{lem:default}
The household's first-order condition with respect to $D \in [0, b_1]$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:foc_D}
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_1}{\partial D} = \lambda_1\!\left(1 - \mf_1 \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}\right).
\end{equation}
The marginal benefit of an additional unit of default is one unit of budget relief. The marginal cost is the induced increase in the domestic import price, weighted by import demand. The full Karush--Kuhn--Tucker conditions are:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:KKT}
\begin{cases}
D = 0 & \text{if } 1 \leq \mf_1 \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}\bigg|_{D=0} \quad \text{\emph{(no default)}},\\[10pt]
1 = \mf_1 \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} & \text{if } D \in (0, b_1) \quad \text{\emph{(partial default)}},\\[10pt]
D = b_1 & \text{if } 1 \geq \mf_1 \dfrac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}\bigg|_{D=b_1} \quad \text{\emph{(full default)}}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
At an interior solution, the household equates the marginal budgetary gain from one extra unit of default with the marginal cost through the external finance premium.
\end{lemma}

\subsection{Transmission of Default to Import Prices}

The mechanism by which sovereign default raises import prices can be traced through the intermediary balance sheet. From the net-worth equation \eqref{eq:nw1}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dpm_dD}
\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} = -\gamma \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}.
\end{equation}
An increase in $D$ erodes intermediary net worth, raising leverage, pushing up the default threshold, increasing the external finance premium, and passing through to the import price:
\[
D \uparrow \;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:nw1}}\; N_1 \downarrow \;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:omega_leverage}}\; \bar{\omega}_1 \uparrow \;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:efp}}\; \frac{Z_1}{\Rstar} \uparrow \;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:pm}}\; p_{m,1} \uparrow \;\xrightarrow{\eqref{eq:mf_demand}}\; \mf_1 \downarrow \;\Longrightarrow\; C_1 \downarrow.
\]

Differentiating \eqref{eq:pm} with respect to $N_1$ at fixed $\Mf_1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dpm_dn}
\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1} =  \Rstar \cdot \frac{-\dfrac{1}{ \Mf_1}\bigl[\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr] - \left(1 - \dfrac{N_1}{ \Mf_1}\right)\!\bigl[\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1) - \mu G'(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]\dfrac{\partial \bar{\omega}_1}{\partial N_1}}{\bigl[\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]^2}.
\end{equation}
This is strictly negative. The first term in the numerator captures the direct reduction in leverage; the second captures the indirect effect through the lowering of $\bar{\omega}_1$, which reduces expected auditing costs. The denominator $[\Gamma - \mu G]^2$ is the source of convexity in the default penalty: as net worth falls in default and $\bar{\omega}$ rises, $\Gamma - \mu G$ shrinks, causing the denominator to contract and the magnitude of $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial N_1$ to accelerate.

To complete the derivation, implicitly differentiating \eqref{eq:omega_leverage} with respect to $N_1$ at fixed $\Mf_1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:domega_dn}
\frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_1}{\partial N_1} = \frac{-\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)\,\bar{\omega}_1 \cdot \dfrac{ \Mf_1}{( \Mf_1 - N_1)^2}}{\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1) + \bigl[\Gamma''(\bar{\omega}_1)\,\bar{\omega}_1 + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]\frac{N_1}{M^f_1-N_1}},
\end{equation}
which is strictly negative provided the second-order conditions of the intermediary's problem are satisfied. Higher net worth lowers the default threshold. The numerator is negative because $\Gamma' > 0$ and $\bar{\omega} > 0$; the denominator is positive by the second-order conditions.

\begin{proposition}[Convexity of the Default Penalty]\label{prop:convexity}
The penalty to the Representative Household of default is convex in $D$: a linear increase in sovereign default triggers a more-than-proportional increase in the domestic import price $p_{m,1}$. This follows from two reinforcing effects as leverage increases:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item $|\partial\bar{\omega}_t / \partial N_t|$ grows, since the numerator of \eqref{eq:domega_dn} expands via the $( \Mf_t - N_t)^2$ term while the denominator contracts;
\item $[\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_t) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_t)]^2$ in the denominator of \eqref{eq:dpm_dn} shrinks as $\bar{\omega}_t$ rises.
\end{enumerate}
Together, $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial D$ is increasing in $D$.
\end{proposition}

% Worth stating here that, whilst convexity pushes against total default, it does not rule it out.

\begin{proposition}[Default increases in the ratio of debt-to-endowment]\label{prop:inv-debt-gdp}
Assume an interior default equilibrium where $D\in(0,b_1)$ and the second-order condition for utility maximization is strictly satisfied. The optimal sovereign default volume $D(y_1, b_1)$ is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Strictly decreasing in the realised Period 1 endowment $y_1$.
    \item Strictly increasing in the Period 1 debt issuance $b_1$.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, the optimal haircut is governed by the household's capacity to pay, increasing alongside the debt-to-endowment ratio $b_1/y_1$.
\begin{proof}
    Let $F(D;y_1,b_1)$ denote the first-order condition for optimal default \eqref{eq:KKT}, under an interior equilibrium. Also, let $W_1\equiv y_1-(b_1-D)+\Pi_1$ denote the household's Period 1 wealth. By Proposition \ref{prop:convexity}, the derivative of $F(.)$ the  with respect to $D$ is strictly negative. Applying the Implicit Function Theorem, the comparative statics of optimal default with respect to the state variable $y_1$ is given by
    \begin{equation*}
        \frac{\partial F(D;y_1,b_1)}{\partial y_1}=-\left( \frac{\partial m^f_1}{\partial W_1} \frac{\partial W_1}{\partial y_1} \right)\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}.
    \end{equation*}
    Since $\frac{\partial W_1}{\partial y_1}=1$, $\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}>0$ and, as $\eta<0$, $\frac{\partial m^f_1}{\partial W_1}>0$, it follows that $\frac{\partial F(D;y_1,b_1)}{\partial y_1}<0$. Therefore $\frac{\partial D}{\partial y_1}<0$.

    Differentiating $F(.)$ with respect to $b_1$ yields
    \begin{equation*}
        \frac{\partial F(D;y_1,b_1)}{\partial b_1} = -\left( \frac{\partial m^f_1}{\partial W_1} \frac{\partial W_1}{\partial b_1} \right)\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}+m^f_1\left( \frac{\partial}{\partial b_1} \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D} \right).
    \end{equation*}
    Due to the construction of claims made in Period 0, $\frac{\partial W_1}{\partial b_1}=-1$, in turn guaranteeing $\frac{\partial m^f_1}{\partial b_1}<0$. As $b_1$ enters positively into the Domestic Intermediary's Period 1 net-worth \eqref{eq:bc1}, from \eqref{eq:domega_dn} we can see that $\frac{\partial}{\partial b_1}\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial D}<0$. These two derivatives mean that $\frac{\partial F}{\partial b_1}>0$, implying $\frac{\partial D}{\partial b_1}>0$.
\end{proof}

% Implication of this here

\end{proposition}

\subsection{Intertemporal Optimality}

\begin{lemma}[Euler Equation]\label{lem:euler}
The household's first-order condition with respect to $b_1$, applying the envelope theorem to $V_1$, yields:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:euler}
\lambda_0\, q_0 = \beta\,\E_0\!\left[\lambda_1\!\left(1 + \gamma\,\mf_1 \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}\right)\right].
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the envelope theorem, the marginal value of an additional unit of debt carried into Period 1 is:
\[
\frac{\partial V_1}{\partial b_1} = \lambda_1\!\left(-1 - \mf_1 \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial b_1}\right).
\]
Since $N_1 = \bar{n} + \gamma(b_1 - D)$ and default $D$ is taken as given at the time of issuance:
\[
\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial b_1} = \gamma \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}.
\]
Note the sign: higher $b_1$ raises intermediary net worth (since banks hold $\gamma b_1$ in bonds), so $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial b_1 < 0$. Combining with the Period 0 first-order condition $\lambda_0 q_0 = \beta \E_0[-\partial V_1 / \partial b_1]$ and simplifying yields \eqref{eq:euler}.
\end{proof}

The left-hand side of \eqref{eq:euler} is the marginal utility cost of purchasing one unit of debt at price $q_0$ in Period 0. The right-hand side comprises two components: the direct repayment of one unit of resources, and the improvement in Period 1 import prices from the additional net worth that $\gamma$ units of bond holdings deliver to intermediaries. Since $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial N_1 < 0$, the term $\gamma \mf_1 (\partial p_{m,1}/\partial N_1)$ is negative. Higher debt issuance strengthens bank balance sheets but also increases the household's future repayment burden. In equilibrium, the household balances the Period 0 consumption benefit of additional borrowing against both the direct repayment cost and the indirect effect on import prices.

\section{Characterisation of Equilibrium}

\subsection{Period 1 Equilibrium}

Take as given the states $(y_1, b_1)$ and the parameter $R^*$. 
Define the following shorthand, which depend only on 
$(D, \bar{\omega}_1, M^f_1)$ and parameters:
\begin{align*}
N_1(D) &\equiv \bar{n} + \gamma(b_1 - D), \\
\ell_1(D, M^f_1) &\equiv 1 - \frac{N_1(D)}{M^f_1}, \\
\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1) &\equiv \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1), \\
p_{m,1}(D, \bar{\omega}_1, M^f_1) &= R^* \cdot 
  \frac{\ell_1}{\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1)}.
\end{align*}
The Period~1 equilibrium values of $(D, \bar{\omega}_1, M^f_1)$ 
solve the following nonlinear system.

\medskip
\textit{Leverage--threshold condition} 
[from \eqref{eq:omega_leverage} and \eqref{eq:nw1}]:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sys_leverage}
1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1) 
= \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)\,\bar{\omega}_1 
  \cdot \frac{N_1(D)}{M^f_1 - N_1(D)}.
\end{equation}

\textit{Foreign input market clearing} 
[from \eqref{eq:mf_demand}, \eqref{eq:bc1}, 
\eqref{eq:mc_foreign}, and \eqref{eq:Pi_agg}]:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sys_clearing}
M^f_1 \bigl[1 - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]
\left[
  \left(\frac{\alpha\, p_{m,1}}{1 - \alpha}\right)^{\!\sigma} 
  + p_{m,1}
\right]
= y_1 - (b_1 - D) 
  + \bigl[1 - \Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]\, p_{m,1}\, M^f_1.
\end{equation}

\textit{Interior default condition} 
[from \eqref{eq:KKT}, \eqref{eq:dpm_dD}, 
and \eqref{eq:dpm_dn}]:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sys_default}
\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1)^2 
= \gamma R^* M^f_1 \bigl[1 - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]
\left(
  \frac{\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1)}{M^f_1}
  + \ell_1 
    \Psi'(\bar{\omega}_1)
    \left\lvert
      \frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_1}{\partial N_1}
    \right\rvert
\right),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \bar{\omega}_1}{\partial N_1}
= \frac{
  -\Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)\,\bar{\omega}_1\, M^f_1
}{
  (M^f_1 - N_1)
  \Bigl[
    \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)(M^f_1 - N_1)
    + N_1 \bigl[\Gamma''(\bar{\omega}_1)\,\bar{\omega}_1 
      + \Gamma'(\bar{\omega}_1)\bigr]
  \Bigr]
}.
\end{equation*} \\

At corner solutions, \eqref{eq:sys_default} is replaced by $D = 0$ or $D = b_1$ with the corresponding inequality 
from the KKT conditions \eqref{eq:KKT}. \\

All other Period~1 variables are determined residually: $N_1$ from 
\eqref{eq:nw1}, $p_{m,1}$ from \eqref{eq:pm}, $m^f_1$ from \eqref{eq:mc_foreign}, $\Pi_1$ from \eqref{eq:Pi_agg}, $m^d_1$ from \eqref{eq:bc1}, $C_1$ from \eqref{eq:CES}, $Z_1$ from \eqref{eq:efp}, and $K_1 = M^f_1 - N_1$. \\

\subsection{Period 0 Equilibrium}

Given Period 1 policy functions $D(y_1,b_1)$, and the associated Period 1 equilibrium mappings, the Period 0 equilibrium values of $(b_1, q_0)$ solve the following system. \\

\textit{Euler equation} [from \eqref{eq:euler}]:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_0\, q_0 = \beta\,\E_0\!\left[\lambda_1\!\left(1 + \gamma\,\mf_1 \frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}\right)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_t$ is derived in \eqref{eq:shadow}.\\

\textit{Intertemporal bond pricing equation} [from \eqref{eq:bondprice}]:
\begin{equation}
    q_0 = \frac{1}{\Rstar}\,\E_0\!\left[1 - \frac{D(y_1, b_1)}{b_1}\right].
\end{equation}

Given a solution, the remaining Period~0 objects are recovered as: $N_0$ from \eqref{eq:nw0}, $\mf_0$ from 
\eqref{eq:mf_demand}, $\md_0$ from \eqref{eq:bc0}, $C_0$ from \eqref{eq:CES}, $\Pi_0$ from 
\eqref{eq:Pi_agg}, and $K_0 = M^f_0 - N_0$. Under the maintained assumption that Period~0 intermediaries are sufficiently capitalised, $\bar{\omega}_0$ and $Z_0$ are pinned down by \eqref{eq:omega_leverage} and \eqref{eq:efp} at negligible values of the external finance premium.

\paragraph{Ruling out no-trade equilibrium} For the rest of the paper, attention will be restricted to the non-trivial interior partial default equilibrium, in which $q_0>0$ and $D\in (0,b_1)$;

\begin{assumption}\label{ass:interior}
Parameters $(\bar{n}, \gamma, \sigma_\omega, \mu, \eta, \alpha, R^*)$ and states $(y_1, b_1)$ are such that the KKT conditions~\eqref{eq:KKT} admit an interior solution $D \in (0, b_1)$.
\end{assumption}

The model admits a second class of equilibria in which the international lender anticipates full default, sets $q_0=0$, and the household is indifferent between not borrowing and borrowing for free with the intention of defaulting upon all claims, as identified by \citet{dubey_default_2005} in models of default with non-contingent debt. The two-period setting presented in this model, without a continuation value from market access, means that the household is exactly indifferent between these two equilibria. I choose to omit the analysis of such an equilibrium, following the practice of \citet{arellano_default_2008}, \citet{peiris_capital_2025} and others, to focus upon the borrowing, default, and intermediary channels that are of primary interest to this thesis.

\section{Comparative Statics and Testable Predictions}\label{sec:implications}

The competitive equilibrium of Definition~\ref{def:equil} features a pecuniary externality: the atomistic household treats the bond price $q_0$ parametrically and therefore does not internalise how its borrowing affects expected default and the cost of debt. To characterise this inefficiency, I define a constrained Ramsey planner's problem, under which there is no coordination failure and thus the effect of default on the cost of borrowing is fully internalised in allocation choices, and compare outcomes with the competitive equilibrium of Definition~\ref{def:equil}. I then show that the sovereign-bank linkage parameter $\gamma$ acts as the primary amplification parameter of interest, governing the severity of default episodes.

\subsection{The Constrained Planner's Problem}\label{sec:planner}

Consider a domestic Ramsey planner who chooses $b_1$ in Period~0 to maximise household welfare, subject to the same Period~1 equilibrium conditions as the competitive economy. That is, the planner takes as given the Period~1 default rule $D(y_1, b_1)$ and the associated competitive equilibrium mappings for $(\bar{\omega}_1, M^f_1, p_{m,1}, C_1)$, but internalises the bond price schedule.
The planner solves:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:planner}
    \max_{b_1 \geq 0} \; u\!\bigl(C_0(b_1)\bigr) + \beta\,\E_0\!\bigl[u\!\bigl(C_1(y_1, b_1)\bigr)\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $C_0$ depends on $b_1$ through the budget constraint \eqref{eq:bc0} evaluated at $q_0(b_1)$:
\[
    m^d_0 = y_0 - b_0 + q_0(b_1)\cdot b_1 + \Pi_0.
\]

\begin{definition}[Constrained Planner's Equilibrium]\label{def:planner_equil}
    A constrained planner's equilibrium is an allocation $\{b_1^{P}, q_0^{P}, D^{P}(y_1)\}$ and associated prices and quantities such that:
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
        \item $b_1^{P}$ solves the planner's problem \eqref{eq:planner};
        \item For each realisation $y_1$, the Period~1 equilibrium $(D, \bar{\omega}_1, M^f_1)$ satisfies the same system \eqref{eq:sys_leverage}--\eqref{eq:sys_default} as under the competitive equilibrium;
        \item The bond price satisfies the \textit{schedule} \eqref{eq:bondprice}.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Whilst the planner's problem is identical to that of the atomistic household in Period 1, the critical distinction from the competitive equilibrium is that the planner perceives the total revenue from bond issuance as
\[
    \mathcal{R}(b_1) \equiv q_0(b_1)\cdot b_1,
\]
and therefore the marginal revenue from issuing an additional unit of debt is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:marginal_revenue}
    \mathcal{R}'(b_1) = q_0(b_1) + q_0'(b_1)\cdot b_1,
\end{equation}
where the competitive household perceives only the first term. The second term, $q_0'(b_1)\cdot b_1$, captures the debt-dilution externality: additional borrowing raises expected default, depresses the bond price, and erodes the revenue raised on \emph{all} outstanding issuance.


\subsection{The Welfare Wedge}\label{sec:wedge}

Differentiating \eqref{eq:planner} with respect to $b_1$ yields the planner's first-order condition:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:planner_foc}
    \lambda_0^{P}\,\mathcal{R}'(b_1) = \beta\,\E_0\!\left[\lambda_1\!\left(1 + \gamma\,m^f_1\,\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}\right) + u'(C_1)\frac{\partial C_1}{\partial D}\frac{\partial D}{\partial b_1}\right],
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_t$ denotes the shadow value of income \eqref{eq:shadow}. 

At the competitive equilibrium allocation, the household's Euler equation \eqref{eq:euler} holds:
\[
    \lambda_0\,q_0 = \beta\,\E_0\!\left[\lambda_1\!\left(1 + \gamma\,m^f_1\,\frac{\partial p_{m,1}}{\partial N_1}\right)\right].
\]
The difference between the planner's and the household's Period~0 optimality conditions identifies a welfare wedge. Evaluating the planner's FOC at the competitive equilibrium allocation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wedge}
    \underbrace{\lambda_0\,q_0'(b_1)\cdot b_1}_{\text{debt-dilution externality}} + \beta\,\E_0\!\left[\underbrace{u'(C_1)\,\frac{\partial C_1}{\partial D}\frac{\partial D}{\partial b_1}}_{\text{insurance value of default}}\right] \neq 0.
\end{equation}

If the competitive equilibrium were constrained efficient, this expression would equal zero and the planner would have no desire to adjust $b_1$. The sign of \eqref{eq:wedge} determines the direction of the inefficiency. The first term is unambiguously negative: $q_0'(b_1) < 0$ because higher debt raises expected default, so the household overborrrows from the perspective of bond revenue. The second term captures the insurance value of partial default under incomplete markets: higher $b_1$ raises $D(y_1, b_1)$ in low-endowment states (Proposition~\ref{prop:inv-debt-gdp}), which, despite the convex cost from Proposition~\ref{prop:convexity}, provides state-contingent relief through the partial default margin.

The insurance term in \eqref{eq:wedge} reflects a valuable property of partial default under incomplete markets. We can think of partial default as expanding the effective asset span of the non-contingent bond. With partial default, the household can smooth its state-contingent repayment, making the non-contingent bond behave more like a portfolio of Arrow securities. The more responsive $D(y_1,b_1)$ is to the state, the better the insurance properties of the debt instrument. Under the CSV friction, however, households are subject to a convex cost associated with this insurance. When the household chooses an interior default solution, we can see from the household's first-order condition \eqref{eq:foc_D} that the household selects $D$ such that $\frac{\partial C_1}{\partial D}=0$, and thus the insurance value of default collapses to 0\footnote{It is worth noting that this does not necessarily hold in corner solutions: the envelope condition from \eqref{eq:foc_D} does not bind, and the sign of the second term of \eqref{eq:wedge}, and thus the direction of borrowing compared to the planner's optimum depends on the magnitude of the CSV friction relative to the intermediary net-worth.}. We can see, then, that households overborrows compared to the planner's optimum.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}\label{sec:predictions}

% The welfare wedge in \eqref{eq:wedge} arises because partial default provides state-contingent insurance that the competitive household undervalues. The existence and structure of this insurance channel — absent from binary default frameworks — generates a set of testable implications for the cross-section of sovereign restructuring episodes. I posit five testable predictions; the first two follow directly from Propositions~\ref{prop:convexity} and~\ref{prop:inv-debt-gdp}; the remaining three exploit the interaction between partial default and the CSV friction. \\

% \textbf{Prediction 1} (Haircuts increase in debt-to-endowment). \emph{In a cross-section of sovereign restructuring episodes, realised haircuts are positively associated with the pre-default debt-to-GDP ratio.}\\

% \textbf{Prediction 2} (Convexity of the output--haircut relationship). \emph{The relationship between haircut severity and the post-default output decline is convex: larger haircuts are associated with disproportionately larger output losses, and this convexity is increasing in the share of sovereign debt held domestically.}\\

% \textbf{Prediction 3} (Direction of borrowing inefficiency depends on financial sector fragility). \emph{The sign of the welfare wedge \eqref{eq:wedge} is governed by the strength of the sovereign–intermediary linkage. For low values of $\gamma$, the insurance value of partial default dominates the debt-dilution externality, and the competitive equilibrium exhibits underborrowing relative to the constrained planner. As $\gamma$ increases, the convex cost of default through the intermediary balance sheet (Proposition~\ref{prop:convexity}) erodes the insurance value, and at sufficiently high $\gamma$ the debt-dilution externality dominates, generating overborrowing.} Empirically, countries with high domestic bank sovereign exposure should exhibit debt-to-GDP ratios that exceed the constrained-efficient level, whereas countries with low exposure should exhibit the opposite. \\
 
% \textbf{Prediction 4} (Import compression is continuous in haircuts). \emph{Across sovereign restructuring episodes, the magnitude of import compression is positively correlated with the realised haircut, and this gradient is steeper for countries with larger banking-sovereign linkages.} Import prices respond via $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial D = -\gamma\,(\partial p_{m,1}/\partial N_1) > 0$ from \eqref{eq:dpm_dD}, and the household's demand for foreign inputs $m^f_1$ is decreasing in $p_{m,1}$ via \eqref{eq:mf_demand}.\\

% \textbf{Prediction 5} (The external finance premium rises with haircut severity). \emph{Post-default corporate borrowing costs increase with the realised haircut, and the relationship is steeper for countries with higher domestic bank sovereign exposure.} The model's external finance premium is $Z_1/R^* = \ell_1/\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1)$ from \eqref{eq:efp} and \eqref{eq:pm}, which is increasing in the default volume $D$ through the intermediary balance sheet channel. A larger haircut erodes $N_1$, raises $\bar{\omega}_1$, and pushes up the premium.

\textbf{Prediction 1} (Haircuts increase in debt-to-endowment). \emph{In a cross-section of sovereign restructuring episodes, realised haircuts are positively associated with the pre-default debt-to-GDP ratio.}\\
 
\noindent\textbf{Prediction 2} (Sovereign spreads increase in domestic bank exposure). \emph{For a given debt-to-GDP ratio, countries with a higher share of sovereign debt held by domestic banks exhibit wider CDS spreads.}
 
This is the empirical counterpart of comparative static~(ii). The doom loop mechanism implies that higher $\gamma$ raises the expected cost of default for any given debt level, which is priced into sovereign bonds via \eqref{eq:bondprice}. \\
 
 
\noindent\textbf{Prediction 3} (Import compression is graded in haircut severity, amplified by $\gamma$). \emph{Across sovereign restructuring episodes, the magnitude of import compression is positively correlated with the realised haircut, and this gradient is steeper for countries with domestic banks that are more exposed to sovereign debt.}
 
This is the empirical counterpart of comparative static~(iii). Import prices respond via $\partial p_{m,1}/\partial D = -\gamma\,(\partial p_{m,1}/\partial N_1) > 0$ from \eqref{eq:dpm_dD}, and household demand for foreign inputs is decreasing in $p_{m,1}$ via \eqref{eq:mf_demand}. In \citet{mendoza_general_2012}, default triggers discrete exclusion from working capital markets. Here, the import declines continuously with the haircut, and the gradient is steeper at higher $\gamma$. \\
 
\noindent\textbf{Prediction 4} (The external finance premium rises continuously with haircut severity, amplified by $\gamma$). \emph{Post-default corporate borrowing costs increase with the realised haircut, and the relationship is steeper for countries with higher domestic bank sovereign exposure.}
 
This is the empirical counterpart of comparative static~(iv). The external finance premium $Z_1/R^* = \ell_1/\Psi(\bar{\omega}_1)$ from \eqref{eq:efp} and \eqref{eq:pm} is increasing in $D$ through the intermediary balance sheet.\\
 
\noindent\textbf{Prediction 5} (The relationship between output and haircut is convex, with convexity increasing in $\gamma$). \emph{The relationship between haircut severity and the post-default output decline is convex: larger haircuts are associated with disproportionately larger output losses, and this convexity is increasing in the share of sovereign debt held domestically.}
 
This is the empirical counterpart of comparative statics~(iii)-(iv) combined with Proposition~\ref{prop:convexity}. The convexity arises because the marginal cost of default accelerates as intermediary net worth falls, driven by the contracting denominator $[\Gamma(\bar{\omega}_1) - \mu G(\bar{\omega}_1)]^2$ in \eqref{eq:dpm_dn}. The interaction with $\gamma$ follows because higher $\gamma$ amplifies the pass-through from $D$ to $N_1$ via \eqref{eq:nw1}. An empirical test would regress the post-default GDP decline on the realised haircut, its square, and an interaction with the domestic bank sovereign holdings share.

\section{Quantitative Analysis}

I now move to examine the quantitative implications of the model by conducting numerical simulations. Under the model's two-period setting, each period is set to a length of five years. This setting seeks to strike a balance between temporal realism and calibration feasibility. Periods shorter than this length do not allow for a realistic time frame we would reasonably expect sovereign renegotiation to occur within. Five years allows Period 1 to capture equilibrium following complete conclusion of refinancing. On the other hand, longer period settings would lead to issues of liquidity in our chosen instrument of calibration - CDS Swaps. 5 year sovereign bond CDS contracts are generally more widely traded than 10 year, thus ensuring implied default probability is adequately captured in price data. Thus, the discount rate and risk-free rate are calibrated to their respective five year compounded values. For the remainder of the quantitative analysis, I set my utility function to a CRRA arrangement,
\begin{equation*}
    u(C_t)=\frac{C_t^{1-\sigma_u}}{1-\sigma_u},
\end{equation*}
where $\sigma_u$ is the coefficient of relative risk aversion.
